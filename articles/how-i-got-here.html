Founding Story

8 Min Read

Deconstructing the noise of expectation to find personal resonance.

I used to look at my transcript with a sense of quiet shame. Three major changes. Three times where I stood in front of an advisor and admitted that the path I was on—the one I had argued for just months prior—no longer felt real. To an outsider, it looked like indecision. To me, it was a psychological survival tactic.

I spent a year within the Management Fellows program, a community designed for the high-achievers of business. On the surface, I was succeeding. I was learning the vernacular of the corporate elite. But inside, I was experiencing a profound cognitive disconnect. I was in a room full of people who knew how to act, but I was the only one paralyzed because I didn't understand why.

"As a cognitive function enthusiast, I realized early on that my brain is wired for 'Introverted Intuition'. I cannot move until I see the pattern. I cannot execute until I find the motive."

The Friction of the "Lost" Era

During this "lost" era, I felt like a machine without an engine. I moved from Economics to Actuarial Science, searching for a language that didn't feel like a costume. I needed a field that honored my natural obsession with patterns and human behavior, but one that was grounded in the absolute truth of logic.

Business felt too abstract—stories without proof. Pure coding felt too cold—syntax without soul. I was looking for the intersection, the motive behind the system. I needed to know why a 2% shift in a specific variable caused a 20% shift in human action.

The moment noise becomes a narrative.

The Homecoming

Everything changed during a conversation with a friend. They were showing me a project involving data visualization, and for the first time, I felt a spark of pure, unforced curiosity. It wasn't just "tech"—it was a magnifying glass for the world.

Internal Realization

Suddenly, the 'Triple Pivot' made sense. I wasn't running away from business; I was searching for the evidence of it. I wasn't running toward CS; I was looking for the tools to prove my intuitions. Data was the bridge.

Data gave me my Big Why. It turned "management" into "optimization." It turned "feeling" into "probability." It allowed my brain to finally operate at full capacity because the motive was built into the methodology: to find the truth hidden in the data.

The Analytics Architect

Today, I call myself an Analytics Architect. I chose this title because it implies both the structure (CS) and the purpose (Business). I don't just want to build dashboards; I want to build the motives that drive them.

I no longer look at my three major changes with regret. They were the iterations of a data scientist before I even knew the term existed. I am no longer lost, because I found the only language that can actually explain the world I see.
